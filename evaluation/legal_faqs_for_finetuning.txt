FAQ-001
Category: Criminal Law
Q: Can police arrest without a warrant?
A: Yes, but ONLY for cognizable offences (listed in the First Schedule of
BNSS). For non-cognizable offences, police need a warrant from a Magistrate.
Even for cognizable offences, per Arnesh Kumar v. State of Bihar (2014),
arrest is not mandatory and should be the last resort for offences punishable
up to 7 years. The police officer must record reasons for arrest.

FAQ-002
Category: Constitutional Law
Q: Can fundamental rights be suspended during an Emergency?
A: Under Article 358, rights under Article 19 are automatically suspended
during a National Emergency proclaimed on grounds of war or external
aggression. Under Article 359, the President may suspend the right to move
courts for enforcement of fundamental rights (except Articles 20 and 21,
which can NEVER be suspended — 44th Amendment, 1978).

FAQ-003
Category: Family Law
Q: What is the legal age of marriage in India?
A: For men: 21 years. For women: 18 years.This is governed by the Prohibition
of Child Marriage Act, 2006. A child marriage is VOIDABLE (not void ab initio)
at the option of the contracting party who was a child at the time. The
Prohibition of Child Marriage (Amendment) Bill, 2021 proposed raising the age
for women to 21, but as of 2025, it has not been enacted.

FAQ-004
Category: Consumer Law
Q: How do I file a consumer complaint?
A: Under the Consumer Protection Act, 2019: (1) Complaints can be filed at
the District Commission (claims up to Rs. 50 lakh), State Commission (Rs. 50
lakh to Rs. 2 crore), or National Commission (above Rs. 2 crore). (2) Can be
filed online through e-daakhil portal (edaakhil.nic.in). (3) No lawyer is
required — consumer can appear in person. (4) Must be filed within 2 years of
the cause of action. (5) Court fee is nominal (Rs. 100-5000 depending on
claim value).

FAQ-005
Category: Property Law
Q: What is the difference between a Sale Deed and an Agreement to Sell?
A: A Sale Deed (Section 54, Transfer of Property Act) is a COMPLETED transfer
of ownership — title passes immediately upon execution and registration. An
Agreement to Sell (Section 54, Indian Contract Act) is a PROMISE to sell at a
future date — title does NOT pass, only a personal right to purchase is
created. An Agreement to Sell creates no interest or charge on the property.
If the seller breaches, the buyer can only claim specific performance through
court, not automatic ownership.

FAQ-006
Category: Labour Law
Q: What is the maximum working hours permitted under Indian law?
A: Under the Occupational Safety, Health and Working Conditions Code, 2020
(when fully enforced), the maximum is 8 hours per day. Under the Factories
Act, 1948 (still applicable in many states): 48 hours per week, maximum 9
hours per day. Overtime pay must be at TWICE the ordinary rate of wages.
The Code on Wages, 2019 also mandates that no worker shall be required to
work for more than the prescribed hours in any day.

FAQ-007
Category: Cyber Law
Q: Is sharing someone's private photos without consent a crime?
A: Yes, under multiple provisions: (1) Section 66E of IT Act — imprisonment
up to 3 years and fine up to Rs. 2 lakh for publishing private images without
consent; (2) Under BNS, 2023 — Section 77 (voyeurism) punishes watching or
capturing private acts, imprisonment up to 3 years for first offence and up
to 7 years for repeat offence; (3) Section 67/67A of IT Act — publishing
obscene/sexually explicit material; (4) POCSO Act if victim is a minor.

FAQ-008
Category: RTI
Q: Can I file an RTI application to get information about a private company?
A: Generally NO — RTI Act, 2005 applies only to "public authorities" (Section
2(h)): government bodies, bodies established by Constitution/statute, bodies
owned/controlled/substantially financed by government. However, a private
company can be compelled to provide information INDIRECTLY if: (a) the
information is held by a public authority that regulates the company, or
(b) the private company receives substantial government funding (Section 2(h)).

FAQ-009
Category: Tax Law
Q: Is agricultural income taxable in India?
A: Agricultural income is EXEMPT from income tax under Section 10(1) of the
Income Tax Act, 1961, as per the constitutional division of powers (Entry 82,
Union List excludes agricultural income). However: (1) Agricultural income is
used for RATE PURPOSES if non-agricultural income exceeds the basic exemption
limit; (2) State governments can levy agricultural income tax; (3) Income from
agricultural land sold within city limits may attract capital gains tax; (4)
Processing of agricultural produce resulting in a marketable commodity may be
treated as business income, not agricultural income.

FAQ-010
Category: Criminal Law
Q: What is anticipatory bail and how is it different from regular bail?
A: Anticipatory bail (Section 482 BNSS / old Section 438 CrPC) is bail
granted BEFORE arrest — a person who apprehends arrest can apply to the
Sessions Court or High Court for a direction that if arrested, he shall be
released on bail. Regular bail (Section 483 BNSS / old Section 439 CrPC) is
applied for AFTER arrest. Key differences: (1) Anticipatory bail is pre-arrest;
regular bail is post-arrest; (2) Anticipatory bail can be granted by Sessions
Court or High Court only; regular bail can be granted by any court; (3)
Anticipatory bail may be conditional and limited in duration; (4) Not available
in all offences (e.g., some states restrict it for certain BNS offences).

FAQ-011
Category: Family Law
Q: Who gets custody of children after divorce?
A: Indian courts apply the "BEST INTERESTS OF THE CHILD" principle. Generally:
(1) Children below 5 years — mother gets custody (Guardians and Wards Act,
Section 6); (2) Above 5 years — courts consider child's welfare holistically;
(3) Child above 9 years — court gives weight to child's preference; (4) Father
typically gets visitation rights; (5) Non-custodial parent usually pays
maintenance. Under Hindu Minority and Guardianship Act: natural guardian of
minor Hindu boy/girl below 5 is mother; above 5 is father.

FAQ-012
Category: Property Law
Q: Can an NRI buy property in India?
A: Yes, an NRI (Non-Resident Indian) can buy: (1) Residential property — no
limit on number; (2) Commercial property — no limit; (3) Agricultural land,
farmhouse, plantation property — CANNOT be purchased (requires RBI approval).
An NRI can inherit any property including agricultural land. Payment must be
made through NRE/NRO account or FCNR account. The property can be sold — if
to a resident Indian, proceeds can be repatriated; if residential property,
up to 2 properties' sale proceeds can be repatriated.

FAQ-013
Category: Arbitration
Q: Can an arbitration award be challenged in court?
A: Yes, under Section 34 of the Arbitration Act, 1996, an arbitral award can
be challenged within 3 MONTHS (extendable by 30 days) on limited grounds:
(1) Party was under incapacity; (2) Arbitration agreement was invalid; (3)
No proper notice was given; (4) Award deals with disputes beyond scope; (5)
Tribunal composition not as agreed; (6) Subject-matter not arbitrable; (7)
Award conflicts with public policy of India (including patent illegality for
domestic awards). Courts cannot review the merits of the award. The scope of
"public policy" has been narrowed after the 2015 Amendment.

FAQ-014
Category: Constitutional Law
Q: What is Public Interest Litigation (PIL)?
A: PIL is a legal action initiated in a court of law for the enforcement of
public interest or general interest, where the public or a class of the
community has a legal interest. Key features: (1) Any person can file, not
just the aggrieved party; (2) Can be filed in High Court (Article 226) or
Supreme Court (Article 32); (3) Even a postcard/letter can be treated as a
PIL; (4) Court fee is minimal; (5) Originated in Hussainara Khatoon v. Bihar
(1979); (6) Cannot be used for personal gain — frivolous PILs attract costs;
(7) PIL is a tool for social justice and judicial activism.

FAQ-015
Category: Insolvency
Q: What happens when a company goes through insolvency proceedings?
A: Under IBC, 2016: (1) CIRP (Corporate Insolvency Resolution Process) is
initiated when a default of Rs. 1 crore or more occurs; (2) An Interim
Resolution Professional (IRP) is appointed; (3) A MORATORIUM is declared —
no suits can be filed against the company; (4) A Committee of Creditors (CoC)
is formed; (5) Resolution plans are invited — must be completed within 330
DAYS; (6) If no resolution plan is approved → liquidation; (7) During CIRP,
the management is vested in the IRP/RP; (8) Existing management is suspended.

FAQ-016
Category: RERA
Q: What should a homebuyer check before buying a flat under RERA?
A: (1) RERA Registration Number — verify on State RERA website; (2) Approved
layout plan and building plan; (3) Carpet area as defined under RERA (not
built-up or super built-up); (4) Delivery timeline committed by developer;
(5) Agreement should be as per RERA model agreement; (6) Check for any pending
complaints against the developer on RERA portal; (7) Land title and
encumbrance certificate; (8) Environmental clearances; (9) Escrow account
details (70% of collections must be in separate account); (10) Track record
of the developer for previous projects.

FAQ-017
Category: Domestic Violence
Q: Can a man file a domestic violence case?
A: Under the Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act, 2005, ONLY
WOMEN can file a complaint as "aggrieved person." A man cannot file under
the DV Act. However, a man can seek remedies under: (1) Section 498A
BNS — if his parents are subjected to cruelty by his wife; (2) Section 323
BNS — for causing hurt; (3) Civil remedies — injunction, damages; (4) Section
125 CrPC/Section 144 BNSS — maintenance. The law has been critiqued for not
being gender-neutral. Some High Courts have recommended gender-neutral DV law.

FAQ-018
Category: GST
Q: Who needs to register for GST?
A: Registration is mandatory if: (1) Annual aggregate turnover exceeds Rs. 40
lakh (Rs. 20 lakh for special category states); (2) For service providers:
Rs. 20 lakh (Rs. 10 lakh for special category states); (3) MANDATORY regardless
of turnover for: inter-state suppliers, casual taxable persons, persons liable
to pay tax under reverse charge, e-commerce operators, input service
distributors, non-resident taxable persons, TDS/TCS deductors, and agents of
suppliers. Composition scheme is available for turnover up to Rs. 1.5 crore
(services up to Rs. 50 lakh).

FAQ-019
Category: Evidence Law
Q: Is a confession given to a police officer admissible?
A: Under Section 22 of BSA, 2023 (formerly Section 25 of Evidence Act), a
confession made to a police officer is NOT ADMISSIBLE. Under Section 23 of
BSA (formerly Section 26), a confession made while in police custody is also
not admissible UNLESS made in the immediate presence of a Magistrate. However:
(1) Information leading to discovery of facts during investigation IS
admissible under Section 24 BSA (formerly Section 27); (2) Confession recorded
by a Magistrate under Section 183 BNSS (formerly Section 164 CrPC) is
admissible; (3) Under Section 15 of TADA/MCOCA, confessions to senior police
officers are admissible (special statutes).

FAQ-020
Category: Data Protection
Q: What are my rights as a data principal under the DPDP Act, 2023?
A: Under the DPDP Act, 2023, you have the following rights: (1) RIGHT TO
INFORMATION — know what personal data is being processed, purpose, and with
whom it's shared (Section 11(a)); (2) RIGHT TO CORRECTION AND ERASURE —
request correction of inaccurate data and erasure of data (Section 11(b));
(3) RIGHT TO GRIEVANCE REDRESSAL — approach the Data Fiduciary's grievance
redressal mechanism and then the Data Protection Board (Section 11(c)); (4)
RIGHT TO NOMINATE — nominate any person to exercise rights in case of your
death or incapacity (Section 11(d)). You also have duties: not to file false
complaints and to provide only authentic information.

FAQ-021
Category: Motor Vehicle Law
Q: What compensation can accident victims claim under the Motor Vehicles Act?
A: Under Section 163A of the Motor Vehicles Act, 1988, compensation is
determined by the Multiplier Method: Annual income × Multiplier (based on
age) + conventional amounts for pain/suffering, loss of consortium, funeral
expenses. No-fault liability under Section 163A provides up to Rs. 50,000
for death and Rs. 25,000 for permanent disablement WITHOUT proving fault.
Claims are filed before the Motor Accident Claims Tribunal (MACT). In
National Insurance Co. v. Pranay Sethi (2017), SC standardized the amounts.

FAQ-022
Category: Criminal Procedure
Q: What is the procedure when someone dies in police custody?
A: Under Section 176 BNSS (formerly Section 176 CrPC): (1) Judicial Magistrate
or Metropolitan Magistrate must conduct an INQUIRY; (2) The inquiry is
MANDATORY — not discretionary; (3) The body must be sent for post-mortem;
(4) The National Human Rights Commission (NHRC) must be informed within 24
hours; (5) Videography of the post-mortem is mandatory; (6) An FIR under
Section 103 BNS (murder) / 105 BNS (culpable homicide) should be considered;
(7) D.K. Basu v. State of West Bengal (1997) guidelines must be followed:
arrest memo, medical examination, right to inform family, etc.

FAQ-023
Category: Environmental Law
Q: What approvals are needed before starting a construction project?
A: (1) Environmental Clearance from SEIAA/MoEFCC under EIA Notification, 2006
for listed projects; (2) CTE (Consent to Establish) and CTO (Consent to
Operate) from State Pollution Control Board under Air Act & Water Act; (3)
RERA Registration if residential project >500 sq.m or >8 apartments; (4)
Building plan approval from local municipal authority; (5) Fire NOC; (6)
Airport Authority NOC (if near airport); (7) CRZ clearance if in Coastal
Regulation Zone; (8) Forest clearance under Forest Conservation Act if forest
land involved; (9) National Green Tribunal compliance certificate.

FAQ-024
Category: Contract Law
Q: When can a contract be considered "void"?
A: A contract is void under the Indian Contract Act, 1872 if: (1) Both parties
are under mistake of fact (Section 20); (2) Consideration or object is
unlawful — immoral, opposed to public policy, or forbidden by law (Section
23); (3) Agreement without consideration (Section 25, with exceptions); (4)
Agreement in restraint of marriage (Section 26); (5) Agreement in restraint of
trade (Section 27, with exceptions); (6) Agreement in restraint of legal
proceedings (Section 28, with exceptions); (7) Agreement the meaning of which
is uncertain (Section 29); (8) Wagering agreement (Section 30); (9) Agreement
to do impossible act (Section 56 — supervening impossibility).

FAQ-025
Category: Cross-Domain
Q: If someone files a false FIR against me, what legal remedies do I have?
A: (1) QUASHING of FIR under Section 528 BNSS (formerly Section 482 CrPC) —
approach the High Court to quash the FIR if it discloses no cognizable
offence; (2) File COUNTER-FIR under Section 211 BNS (false charge of offence)
— imprisonment up to 2 years; (3) File DEFAMATION case under Section 356 BNS
(criminal defamation) — imprisonment up to 2 years; (4) File CIVIL SUIT for
damages (malicious prosecution); (5) File complaint under Section 182 BNS
(false information to public servant) — imprisonment up to 6 months; (6)
Approach the NATIONAL/STATE HUMAN RIGHTS COMMISSION if police harassment; (7)
Seek ANTICIPATORY BAIL under Section 482 BNSS to avoid arrest.
